Signal vs. Telegram: Which Is Actually Private?

Both apps have reputations for privacy. One of them deserves it. The other is coasting on a reputation built on a misunderstanding of what it actually does with your messages.

By Staff Writer  ·  March 2026  ·  6 min read

This comparison comes up constantly, and it frustrates me a little, because the framing of "Signal vs. Telegram" treats them as roughly equivalent alternatives when they're not. They have very different security models. One was designed from the ground up around a specific threat model — government and corporate access to your messages. The other was designed around features, growth, and a founder with specific political circumstances that made a privacy reputation useful.

That's a bit harsh, so let me be more precise.

How Signal Works

Signal uses end-to-end encryption by default for everything — every message, every call, every group chat. The encryption is based on the Signal Protocol, which is also used by WhatsApp and the encryption layer in Facebook Messenger. It's the most widely reviewed messaging encryption protocol in existence, and the review results have been good.

End-to-end means the message is encrypted on your device before it leaves, and decrypted only on the recipient's device. Signal's servers relay the encrypted blob, but they can't read it. Signal has received subpoenas from law enforcement and their response has consistently been: we have almost nothing. A timestamp of when your account was created and when it last connected to their servers. That's it. They can't hand over messages because they don't have them.

Signal is also open source and has been independently audited multiple times. You don't have to trust Signal the company — you can verify what the code actually does.

How Telegram Actually Works

Here's the part that surprises people: Telegram's normal chats — group chats, channels, regular one-on-one messages — are not end-to-end encrypted. They're encrypted in transit, between your device and Telegram's servers, but Telegram's servers can read your messages. The content sits on their infrastructure in a way that could be accessed by Telegram, compelled by courts, or exposed if their systems are compromised.

Telegram does have a "Secret Chat" feature that uses end-to-end encryption. But it's opt-in, not the default, which means it's what virtually nobody uses because nobody realizes they need to turn it on. Secret chats are also device-specific, meaning they don't sync across your other devices, and they don't support groups.

Why Does Telegram Have a Privacy Reputation Then?

Partly history — Telegram was founded by Pavel Durov after he was forced out of VKontakte, Russia's largest social network, under pressure from the Kremlin. He built Telegram partly as a response to that experience, marketed it explicitly as uncensorable and surveillance-resistant, and that framing stuck even though the technical reality of its encryption was always more limited than the marketing implied.

Partly because it genuinely is more resistant to certain kinds of censorship than WhatsApp or iMessage — governments can and do order app stores to remove apps, but the Telegram client is installable directly and its channels have hosted a lot of content that mainstream platforms wouldn't allow. That's a different property than encryption, but it gets bundled together in people's mental model of "privacy."

When to Use Each

Use Signal if your concern is the content of your communications being read — by your carrier, by a platform, by law enforcement with a warrant, by anyone who compromises a server somewhere. Signal is the right tool for actual sensitive conversations.

Telegram is genuinely useful for other things: large communities, public channels, bots, and features that Signal has no equivalent for. Plenty of people use it for exactly those things without any illusion that their group chats are private. That's a reasonable use case.

The problem is the number of people who use Telegram thinking it's like Signal. If you're in a chat group discussing something you'd prefer to keep private, and you haven't specifically switched to Secret Chats — which, again, almost nobody does in practice — your messages are readable by Telegram's servers. That's a real gap between perception and reality, and it's worth understanding before you decide where to have sensitive conversations.
