Home Assistant: Take Back Control of Your Smart Home

Your smart home probably runs on four company clouds, seven apps, and the hope that nobody goes bankrupt or changes their API. Home Assistant fixes that. Here's the setup.

By Staff Writer  ·  March 2026  ·  9 min read

Smart home technology has a dirty secret: almost everything in it is a subscription waiting to happen, a cloud service waiting to be discontinued, or a company waiting to be acquired by someone who'll change the terms. The Wink Hub shutdown in 2020, SmartThings' forced platform migration, Insteon's sudden collapse in 2022 — these aren't anomalies. They're a pattern.

Home Assistant is the answer to that pattern. It's open-source, runs on hardware you own, works locally without internet connectivity, and supports over 3,000 integrations with devices from every manufacturer. When Philips Hue decides to add a subscription, or your thermostat maker gets acquired, Home Assistant doesn't care — it talks to the devices directly.

What You Need to Run It

The recommended hardware is a Raspberry Pi 4 (4GB) or the official Home Assistant Green device (~$100, plug-and-play). You can also run it in a VM or Docker container on existing hardware, but for a dedicated home automation hub, something small and always-on makes more sense.

The easiest installation method is Home Assistant OS, which turns the entire device into a HA appliance:

  1. Download the Home Assistant OS image for your hardware from home-assistant.io/installation
  2. Flash it to an SD card or USB drive using the Balena Etcher tool
  3. Boot your device from the flashed card/drive
  4. Wait a few minutes, then open http://homeassistant.local:8123 in a browser

The onboarding wizard handles the rest — creating your account, setting your location (for sunrise/sunset automations), and detecting devices on your network.

Integrations: Where HA Gets Powerful

The first thing most people do after setup is connect their existing devices. Go to Settings → Devices & Services → Add Integration. You'll find integrations for:

  • Philips Hue (connects locally, no cloud required)
  • Google Nest, Ecobee, and most smart thermostats
  • IKEA Tradfri, Sengled, and other Zigbee lights (requires a Zigbee coordinator dongle)
  • Sonos, Apple Music, Spotify
  • Tesla, Rivian, and most EV integrations
  • Ring, Nest Doorbell, Eufy (camera integration)
  • Anything compatible with Matter (the new smart home standard)

Once devices are connected, they all live in one interface. Your Hue lights and your IKEA bulbs are in the same dashboard. Your thermostat, your TV, and your doorbell are all in the same automation engine.

"The moment all your devices are in one interface is when you understand what Home Assistant actually is. It's not a fancy app — it's a platform."

Automations: The Actual Point

Automations are rules that run automatically based on triggers. The UI-based automation editor handles most common cases without any code:

  • Turn off all lights when everyone leaves home (using phone presence detection)
  • Dim the living room lights at sunset
  • Send a notification if the garage door is left open for more than 30 minutes
  • Start the robot vacuum when the last person leaves
  • Turn on the outdoor lights when motion is detected, but only between sunset and 11pm

For more complex automations, HA has a YAML-based automation format and a scripting language. The community forums and documentation are excellent — whatever you want to automate, someone has probably done it and posted the config.

Local vs. Cloud: The Important Distinction

Some integrations work entirely locally — your hub communicates with the device directly on your local network, no internet required. These are more reliable and faster. Philips Hue, Z-Wave, Zigbee, and Matter devices generally work locally.

Others require a cloud connection — they route through the manufacturer's servers even when the device is sitting on your network. These are less reliable (they break when the manufacturer's API has issues) and are the ones most likely to be discontinued.

In HA, you can see which integrations use cloud and which are local. Gradually migrating to local-capable devices is one of the projects that makes the homelab community happy because it turns smart home devices from "I hope this company stays in business" to "I own this hardware and it works independently."
