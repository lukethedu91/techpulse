Inside the Great Chip Reshoring: Is the TSMC Arizona Bet Paying Off?

The CHIPS Act promised a domestic semiconductor renaissance. TSMC Arizona is now in production. The results are complicated — better than pessimists expected, harder than optimists hoped.

By Staff Writer  ·  March 2026  ·  9 min read

The conventional wisdom on TSMC Arizona went through several phases. First, cautious optimism (2020-2022). Then skepticism, as construction delays, cost overruns, and reports of cultural friction between TSMC's Taiwanese management and American workers dominated the coverage (2022-2024). Now, with Fab 21 Phase 1 in volume production, it's time for something rarer in tech journalism: an honest assessment of results.

The short version: yields are lower than TSMC's Taiwan fabs, costs are higher, but the performance gap is narrowing and the geopolitical insurance policy this represents has a value that's hard to put on a per-chip basis.

The Production Reality

Fab 21 Phase 1 is producing 4nm chips (TSMC's N4 process) primarily for Apple and AMD. Phase 2, targeting 2nm (N2), is under construction and expected to reach volume production in 2026-2027.

Yield rates — the percentage of manufactured chips that pass quality tests — are reportedly in the 75-85% range at Arizona compared to 90%+ at equivalent Taiwan fabs. In semiconductor manufacturing, that gap is meaningful and translates directly to cost per usable chip. Apple has reportedly been accepting Arizona chips for some Mac and iPhone production, but the split remains weighted toward Taiwan for flagship products where per-unit economics matter most.

The reasons for the yield gap are structural and stubborn. Semiconductor manufacturing is extraordinarily sensitive to environmental conditions, process consistency, and what the industry calls "tribal knowledge" — the accumulated practical understanding of thousands of engineers who've been running the same processes for years. Transplanting a fabrication node to a new location with new workers, new supply chains, and a different climate means rebuilding that knowledge base from scratch. It takes years.

The Workforce Problem

The cultural friction that generated headlines in 2023 was real. TSMC's management culture is hierarchical and process-driven in ways that clashed with American workers accustomed to more collaborative norms. Reports of mandatory overtime, Taiwanese managers being sent home for not getting results fast enough, and American engineers feeling sidelined were consistent enough across sources to take seriously.

TSMC has reportedly adjusted. More American managers have been promoted into senior roles, training programs have been expanded, and the first cohort of Arizona-trained engineers now has two years of on-the-job experience at the facility. The 2023 friction doesn't appear to be the ongoing story it was at peak coverage.

"The yield gap between Arizona and Taiwan is real. So is the question of what happens if the Taiwan Strait closes."

The Geopolitical Argument

The case for TSMC Arizona was never primarily economic. The economic case for building chips in the US versus Taiwan is weak on the numbers — labor costs, energy costs, and supply chain proximity all favor Taiwan. The case is about concentration risk.

Taiwan produces the vast majority of the world's leading-edge semiconductor manufacturing capacity. TSMC alone accounts for well over half of global foundry revenue and an even larger share of advanced nodes below 7nm. China considers Taiwan part of its territory. The possibility of a conflict that disrupts Taiwanese semiconductor production is not zero, and the economic and military consequences of losing access to those chips would be severe in ways that the COVID chip shortage only gestured at.

Against that backdrop, paying a 20-30% premium for some chips manufactured in Arizona looks different. It's not a manufacturing strategy — it's an insurance policy with a specific risk scenario in mind.

Intel, Samsung, and the Broader Picture

TSMC isn't the only CHIPS Act beneficiary building new capacity. Intel is investing billions in Ohio and Arizona fabs under its IDM 2.0 strategy, with plans to reach cutting-edge process nodes by 2025-2026. Samsung is building in Texas. Micron is expanding DRAM production in Idaho and New York.

The full picture, if these investments come to fruition, would represent a meaningful diversification of advanced semiconductor manufacturing geography — not enough to eliminate concentration risk, but enough to provide resilience against specific disruption scenarios.

Whether the investments come to fruition is not guaranteed. Semiconductor fab construction is enormously capital-intensive, technically complex, and sensitive to technology roadmap execution. Intel's manufacturing ambitions in particular have a history of optimistic timelines meeting difficult reality. But the direction of travel — more geographic diversity in advanced chip manufacturing — seems durably underway regardless of which specific bets pay off and which don't.
