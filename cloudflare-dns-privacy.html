Change Your DNS to Cloudflare 1.1.1.1 — It Takes Two Minutes

DNS is the phonebook your computer uses every time you visit a website. Your ISP runs the default one, logs the lookups, and sells the data. This is fixable in two minutes.

By Staff Writer  ·  March 2026  ·  4 min read

When you type a URL in your browser, the first thing your computer does is ask a DNS server: "what's the IP address for this domain?" Your internet provider sets the default DNS server, and in most countries that means they see every domain lookup you make — a fairly detailed record of your browsing habits.

ISPs in the United States, the UK, and most of Europe have sold DNS query data to advertisers, used it for targeted advertising, and in some cases injected ads into pages served over HTTP. This is legal in most jurisdictions. It's also avoidable.

Why 1.1.1.1 Specifically

Cloudflare launched 1.1.1.1 in 2018 with a specific privacy commitment: they don't log IP addresses, they don't sell query data, and they have their no-logging policy audited by KPMG annually. The audit results are public. You can decide how much you trust a corporate privacy policy, but it's meaningfully more than the average ISP offers.

Speed-wise, 1.1.1.1 is consistently one of the fastest DNS resolvers globally, outperforming ISP DNS servers in most locations. The DNS Benchmark tool (Gibson Research) will measure your actual latency to various resolvers if you want to verify this for your location.

If you're more skeptical of Cloudflare specifically (they see a large fraction of internet traffic through their CDN business), the alternatives worth considering are Quad9 (9.9.9.9, run by a nonprofit, blocks malware domains) and NextDNS (customizable, free tier, blocks ads and trackers at DNS level).

How to Switch

On your router (affects every device on your network):

Log into your router admin panel (usually 192.168.1.1 or 192.168.0.1). Find DHCP settings or DNS settings — the exact location varies by router manufacturer. Set the primary DNS to 1.1.1.1 and the secondary to 1.0.0.1. Save and restart the router. Done.

On Windows:

Settings → Network & Internet → your connection → DNS server assignment → Edit → Manual → enter 1.1.1.1 and 1.0.0.1.

On Mac:

System Settings → Network → your connection → Details → DNS → add 1.1.1.1 and 1.0.0.1, remove the existing entries.

On iPhone:

Settings → Wi-Fi → tap the (i) next to your network → Configure DNS → Manual → add 1.1.1.1. This only applies to Wi-Fi networks one at a time, which is annoying. For always-on DNS on iPhone, use the Cloudflare 1.1.1.1 app instead, which sets up a local VPN configuration that routes DNS through 1.1.1.1 on all networks.

On Android:

Settings → Network & Internet → Private DNS → enter one.one.one.one. This uses DNS-over-TLS, which also encrypts the DNS queries so even your ISP can't see them as plain text.

"Your ISP sees every domain you look up. This takes two minutes to change."

DNS Over HTTPS and DNS Over TLS

Standard DNS queries are unencrypted — anyone between you and the DNS server (your ISP, a network admin, a man-in-the-middle) can see what you're looking up. DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH) and DNS-over-TLS (DoT) fix this by encrypting the DNS query.

Firefox and Chrome both have DoH built in — enable it in settings and set the provider to Cloudflare. The Android Private DNS setting above uses DoT. If you set DNS at the router level, consider using a router firmware that supports DoH (newer OpenWrt versions do) to encrypt DNS for your whole network.
