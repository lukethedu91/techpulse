Set Up Tailscale in 10 Minutes — Even on Your Router

Traditional VPNs make you deal with port forwarding, static IPs, certificates, and config files. Tailscale makes all of that disappear. Your devices just find each other, no matter what network they're on.

By Staff Writer  ·  March 2026  ·  7 min read

I used to maintain a WireGuard server on a $5 VPS just to securely reach my home network when I was traveling. It worked fine until it didn't — key rotations, firewall rules, the occasional moment where I'd fat-fingered a config file and locked myself out. It was manageable but it was also friction I didn't enjoy.

Tailscale replaced all of that. I installed it on my laptop, my phone, my home server, and my router. They all found each other automatically. I haven't touched the config since.

What Tailscale Actually Does

Tailscale creates a private mesh network — what they call a "tailnet" — across all your devices. Every device gets a stable IP address in the 100.x.x.x range that works regardless of what network you're physically on. Your laptop at a coffee shop and your NAS at home can talk to each other directly as if they're on the same local network.

Under the hood, it uses WireGuard for the actual encrypted tunnels and a coordination server (Tailscale's infrastructure) for discovery and key management. The hard part of WireGuard — managing keys, NAT traversal, making peers find each other — is handled for you. You get the performance and security of WireGuard without any of the administration.

The free tier covers 100 devices and 3 users, which is more than enough for personal use.

Step 1: Create an Account and Install

Go to tailscale.com and sign up — you can use Google, Microsoft, GitHub, or Apple SSO, which means no new password to manage. Then install the client on each device you want to connect:

  • Mac: Download from tailscale.com or the Mac App Store
  • Windows: Standard installer from tailscale.com
  • Linux: One command: curl -fsSL https://tailscale.com/install.sh | sh
  • iOS/Android: Available in both app stores

After installing on each device, click the Tailscale icon and log in with the same account. That's the whole setup. The device appears in your admin console, and within seconds it can reach every other device on your tailnet by its 100.x IP address or its machine name.

Step 2: Test That It's Working

On any two connected devices, just try pinging one from the other by machine name:

ping my-desktop.tail12345.ts.net

Or use the 100.x address you'll find in your admin console. If it responds, you're done — your devices are connected. From this point, you can SSH into your home server by name, access a local web service, mount a network drive, anything you'd do on a local network.

Step 3: Add a Subnet Router (Optional but Useful)

This is where Tailscale gets genuinely powerful. If you install it on a machine that lives on your home network — a Raspberry Pi, your home server, or a router — you can advertise that machine as a subnet router. This means every device on your tailnet can reach your entire home network, not just the devices with Tailscale installed.

On the machine that's always on your home network, run:

tailscale up --advertise-routes=192.168.1.0/24

Replace 192.168.1.0/24 with your home network's actual subnet. Then approve it in the Tailscale admin console under the machine's settings. Now when you're traveling, you can reach your printer, your NAS, your router's admin interface — anything on your home network — without any additional configuration.

"I haven't touched the config in eight months. It just works."

Step 4: Install on an OpenWrt Router

If your router runs OpenWrt (many do, or can be flashed to), you can install Tailscale directly on the router itself. This turns your entire home network into something accessible from anywhere without needing a dedicated always-on machine.

opkg update
opkg install tailscale
tailscale up

After that, approve it in your console and advertise the LAN subnet as above. The router handles it at the network level — every device on your home network is now reachable.

MagicDNS: The Feature That Changes Everything

Enable MagicDNS in your Tailscale admin console. Once it's on, your devices are reachable by name — not just 100.x.x.x addresses but human-readable names like my-nas or work-laptop. It handles DNS resolution across your tailnet automatically.

Combined with subnet routing, this means you can open a browser on your phone while traveling, type http://synology in the address bar, and reach your NAS at home. No IP address to remember, no VPN toggle to flip, nothing — it just works because Tailscale is running in the background.

Exit Nodes: Using It as a Traditional VPN

If you want Tailscale to route all your traffic through a specific machine — for example, to make your laptop's traffic appear to come from your home IP — you can designate any device as an exit node.

tailscale up --advertise-exit-node

Then on any other device, you select that machine as your exit node in the Tailscale app. All your traffic routes through it. This is useful when you're on untrusted public wifi, or when you need to access geo-restricted services that are available at your home location.

Traditional VPN setups take an afternoon to configure correctly, require ongoing maintenance, and fail in ways that are annoying to debug. Tailscale takes ten minutes, and when it works — which is essentially always — it's invisible. That's the goal with networking tools.
