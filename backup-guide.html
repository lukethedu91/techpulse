Back Up Your Digital Life This Weekend

Most people don't think about backups until they lose something. A solid backup system takes one afternoon to set up and then runs silently in the background. Here's the whole plan.

By Staff Writer  ·  March 2026  ·  7 min read

A hard drive failing isn't a question of if. The BACKBLAZE annual hard drive stats report (they operate tens of thousands of drives in their data centers and publish failure rates annually) consistently shows that drives start failing at meaningful rates between years three and five. SSDs have different failure modes but aren't immune. And hardware failure is only one of several ways you can lose data — ransomware, accidental deletion, fire, theft, and the good old "I thought I had a backup" cover all the other cases.

The 3-2-1 rule is old but good: three copies of your data, on two different types of media, with one copy off-site. The redundancy isn't paranoia — it's the acknowledgment that any single failure mode (drive failure, house fire, ransomware that encrypts everything connected to your network) should only be able to take out one of your three copies.

Copy 1: Your Working Files (Already Exists)

This is just whatever's on your computer right now. You already have this. It counts as one copy.

Copy 2: Local Backup to External Drive

Get an external SSD or HDD — 1 TB starts around $60, which is cheaper than the data recovery service you'd need after a drive failure. Then set up automated backup software to run nightly.

On Mac: Time Machine is already installed. Plug in the drive, open System Settings → General → Time Machine, add the drive, done. It backs up hourly while connected and keeps a history of old file versions.

On Windows: Windows Backup (built in) works, but I'd recommend Macrium Reflect Free for a full disk image or FreeFileSync for folder-level backups. Both are free and reliable.

On Linux: rsync is the classic tool:

rsync -avh --delete /home/username/ /media/backup/home/

Or use Timeshift for system snapshots, which works similarly to Time Machine.

Copy 3: Off-Site Cloud Backup

This is the one most people skip and the one that matters most when something genuinely bad happens. Fire, flood, theft, ransomware — a local backup doesn't help you if both your computer and the external drive are in the same place.

Backblaze Personal Backup ($9/month) is the simplest option: install the client, it backs up your entire computer continuously, done. Unlimited storage. Recovery is either download or they'll mail you a drive.

For a cheaper option: Use Restic (free, open source) pointed at Backblaze B2 cloud storage ($6/TB/month). The upfront setup takes about 20 minutes, the ongoing cost is minimal for most personal use, and Restic handles encryption, deduplication, and incremental backups automatically.

# Install restic, initialize a repo on B2
restic -r b2:your-bucket-name:restic init
# Run a backup
restic -r b2:your-bucket-name:restic backup ~/Documents ~/Photos
"An untested backup is not a backup. Run a test restore before you need to depend on it."

The Step Everyone Skips: Test Your Restore

This is non-negotiable. Backup software fails silently sometimes. Drives that look fine might have corrupted data. Storage services go down. The only way to know your backup works is to actually restore something from it.

Once a month, pick a random file from your backup and restore it to a different location. Confirm it opens correctly. Five minutes of verification is the difference between a backup and the illusion of one.

What Doesn't Need to Be Backed Up

Don't back up things you can re-download: installed applications, Steam games, your OS. Focus on irreplaceable things: documents, photos, videos, code repositories, password manager databases, SSH keys. This reduces backup size significantly and makes restores faster.

For code: if it's on GitHub, GitLab, or similar, it's already off-site. You don't need to back it up separately — just make sure you've pushed everything.

The actual work of setting this up is maybe three hours total. The ongoing cost is under $10/month depending on your storage needs. And the peace of mind of knowing that a single catastrophic event can't wipe out years of files is genuinely worth something.
