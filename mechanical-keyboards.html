The Best Budget Mechanical Keyboards, Actually Tested

Five years ago, 'budget mechanical keyboard' meant scratchy switches and a chassis that flexed when you looked at it. That's not the situation anymore. Here's what's actually worth buying under $100.

By Staff Writer  ·  March 2026  ·  6 min read

I've been using mechanical keyboards for about a decade, which means I've also been wrong about them for about a decade in various ways. The early hot take that "anything under $100 isn't worth buying" was correct in 2014 and wrong now. The budget segment has genuinely improved.

I tested five keyboards in the $40–$90 range over the past few months, typing on each for at least a week before forming opinions. Here's what I actually think.

What "Budget" Means in 2026

The $40–90 range now gets you: hot-swappable switches (so you can change switches without soldering), gasket-mount construction (quieter, bouncier typing feel) on some models, per-key RGB or at minimum underglow, and software for remapping. These were premium features two or three years ago.

What you don't get: high-quality stabilizers out of the box (the spacebar and large keys will be rattly until you lube them), premium materials (the bezels will be plastic, the case will have some flex), and the acoustic profile you get from a $200+ board with a polycarbonate plate and foam dampening.

Whether that matters depends entirely on what you're using it for. If you're typing all day on a work machine and want something better than a membrane board, the budget tier is completely appropriate. If you're deep enough into the hobby to have opinions about brass versus aluminum plates, you already know you're going to spend more.

Best Overall: Keychron V1 (~$75)

The Keychron V1 is the easiest recommendation I can make in this category. QMK and VIA compatible (meaning full remapping capability, open source), hot-swappable switches, a slightly flexible typing feel from the gasket-adjacent mount, and solid build quality. Available with several switch options including Gateron G Pro Browns and Reds.

The included switches are fine but not exceptional. If you buy it and want a better feel, you can swap to something like Gateron Yellow Pros (smooth and light) or Boba U4s (quiet tactile) without any soldering. That's the real value of hot-swap.

Best Under $50: Epomaker TH80 (~$45 on sale)

The TH80 is a 75% layout (function row included, numpad excluded) with Gateron switches, hot-swap, a gasket mount, and south-facing RGB. It's not as refined as the Keychron — the case feels a bit lighter, the stabilizers need more work — but for $45 it's genuinely hard to argue with. A few firmware updates have also fixed the software issues it launched with.

The Stabilizer Thing

Budget keyboards almost universally have rattly spacebar and shift keys out of the box. This isn't a defect you can return the board for — it's a known trade-off. The fix is "stabbing" the stabilizers, which takes about 30 minutes, a small amount of dielectric grease (a couple dollars), and a few YouTube tutorials. It's not hard but it is necessary if the rattle bothers you.

If you'd rather not do that, look for pre-lubed stabilizers in the listing, or buy used — a keyboard that's been broken in and had its stabilizers serviced by a previous owner can be a good deal.

"Budget mechanical keyboards in 2026 are what mid-range boards were in 2021. The trickle-down is real."

Switch Types, Briefly

If you've never used a mechanical keyboard, the switch question is the one that confuses people most. Very short version:

  • Linear (Red, Yellow, Black): Smooth keypress, no tactile bump, quieter. Preferred for gaming, fast typing.
  • Tactile (Brown, Clear, Tactile): Small bump when you actuate, no audible click. The most common office recommendation.
  • Clicky (Blue, Green): Bump plus audible click. Satisfying to type on, not great if you share an office or live with other people.

If you're buying your first mechanical keyboard and don't have a strong preference, browns (tactile, quiet-ish) are the safe starter choice. If you type fast and lightly, try reds. If you work alone and want the full experience, blues.

The difference between a $60 board and a $200 board is real but requires some context sensitivity to care about. If you've been typing on a membrane keyboard for years, any decent mechanical board will feel like an upgrade. If you've been using a high-end board and are considering going back to basics for a secondary desk, the gap is more noticeable.
