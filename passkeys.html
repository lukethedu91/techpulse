The End of the Password Era: Passkeys Explained

Passwords were a stopgap that lasted 60 years longer than anyone intended. Passkeys are what replaces them — and the transition is already happening whether you're paying attention or not.

By Staff Writer  ·  March 2026  ·  8 min read

The password has a birthday. It was invented in 1961 at MIT for the Compatible Time-Sharing System, a machine shared among multiple researchers who each needed their own files. The password was a quick fix, intended for a small group of trusted academics on a campus network. Nobody designing it was thinking about 5 billion people sharing a global internet, an entire criminal industry built around credential theft, or the dystopia of remembering 200 different strings of characters for 200 different websites.

And yet here we are, still typing passwords in 2026. Or at least, a lot of people still are. The shift away from them is underway, and if you've signed into an iPhone or a Google account lately, you may have already used a passkey without quite realizing it.

What a Passkey Actually Is

The cleanest way to explain it: a passkey is a pair of cryptographic keys — one stored on your device, one stored on the website. When you log in, the website sends your device a challenge. Your device signs it with the private key. The website verifies that signature with the public key. Done. No password to type, no password to steal, no server-side database full of hashed credentials waiting to be compromised.

The private key never leaves your device. It's protected by your device's secure element — the same hardware chip that stores your Apple Pay or Android biometric data. To access it, you use Face ID, Touch ID, Windows Hello, or your PIN. Not to send anything to the server, but just to authorize the local signing operation.

This matters because phishing — the practice of tricking you into typing your password into a fake website — simply doesn't work against passkeys. There's nothing to type. The private key will only sign challenges from the legitimate domain it was registered with; a convincing-looking fake version of your bank's website gets nothing from your device because cryptography doesn't care how convincing something looks.

Why This Took So Long

The underlying technology — public key cryptography — is decades old. So why are we only now seeing passkeys become mainstream?

The short answer is coordination. For passkeys to work well, your operating system, your browser, your device's hardware security, and every website you want to log into all need to support the same standard. Achieving that across Apple, Google, Microsoft, and the entire web simultaneously requires the kind of multilateral agreement that notoriously takes forever to happen.

The FIDO Alliance (Fast Identity Online) has been pushing on this for years. The WebAuthn standard that underpins passkeys was finalized in 2019. But it wasn't until Apple added passkey sync to iCloud Keychain, Google added it to Password Manager, and both Chrome and Safari made the UX tolerable that the pieces finally clicked together.

"There's nothing to phish. The private key signs the challenge from the legitimate domain only — a fake site gets nothing."

Where Passkeys Work Right Now

As of early 2026, passkeys are supported by most major services. Apple ID, Google, Microsoft, GitHub, Dropbox, PayPal, eBay, Best Buy, Shopify storefronts, Dashlane, 1Password, and most of the large financial institutions have added passkey support. LinkedIn and X added it in late 2024. The holdouts are mostly smaller sites that haven't updated their auth stacks, and they'll get there.

The setup process is usually buried in your security settings — look for "Passkeys" or "Sign-in methods." On most major services, you add a passkey as an additional method, and your password stays as a fallback. Over time you can delete the password.

What About Cross-Device?

The question everyone asks: what happens if I need to sign in on a device that doesn't have my passkey?

Both Apple and Google sync passkeys across your devices using their respective cloud keychains — so your iPhone passkeys are on your iPad and Mac automatically if you're using iCloud Keychain. Windows syncs them across devices signed into the same Microsoft account.

For cross-ecosystem situations — signing into something on your friend's laptop with your iPhone passkey — most implementations let you scan a QR code with your phone, which then handles the authentication via Bluetooth proximity. It sounds clunky but takes about five seconds in practice.

The weak point is account recovery. If you lose all your devices and get locked out of your cloud account, you're in trouble. This is real, and it's the reason passwords haven't disappeared entirely yet. But it's the same problem you already have with any hardware security key, and the recovery flows are improving.

Should You Switch Now?

Yes, where you can. Start with the accounts that matter most — your email, your Apple or Google account, your password manager, your bank if it supports it. These are the ones that cause cascading damage if compromised. Adding a passkey takes maybe three minutes per account and makes them essentially phishing-proof.

You don't have to delete your password immediately. Set up the passkey, use it for a few weeks, and once you're confident in the experience, remove the password. Most people who do this end up preferring passkey login because it's genuinely faster than typing anything.

The password isn't dead yet. But it's been on borrowed time for a while, and the infrastructure to replace it is finally ready. Sixty-five years is a pretty good run for a stopgap.
